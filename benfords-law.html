Benford's Law

The Counter-Intuitive Distribution of First Digits

Imagine you are a forensic accountant auditing a large company. You collect thousands of random expense reports and look at only the very first digit of each expense amount (e.g., $450.00 -> 4, $1,250.00 -> 1).

If the data is completely natural and not fabricated by a fraudster, what percentage of these expenses do you think will start with the digit 1?

Most people assume the leading digits (1-9) are uniformly distributed (~11.1% each). However, in many naturally occurring datasets, the number 1 appears as the first digit about 30.1% of the time!

Why does this happen?

Benford's Law (also known as the First-Digit Law) applies to datasets that span multiple orders of magnitude. Think about a city's population growing: to go from a population starting with 1 (like 10,000) to a population starting with 2 (20,000), it has to grow by 100%.

But to go from a population starting with 9 (90,000) to the next order of magnitude starting with 1 (100,000), it only has to grow by about 11%. Therefore, numbers naturally "spend more time" starting with lower digits before rolling over to the next magnitude.

The expected frequency of digit d (1-9) as the leading digit is given by the formula: log10(1 + 1/d).

This law is actually used in the real world by forensic accountants and the IRS to detect tax fraud, fabricated data, and election anomalies. When humans try to "make up" random numbers, they usually distribute the first digits evenly, which violates Benford's Law and flags the data as suspicious!
