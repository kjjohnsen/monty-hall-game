The False Positive Paradox

Key Terms to Know:
  • Prevalence: The percentage of the population that actually has the disease.
  • Sensitivity (True Positive Rate): If you have the disease, how likely is the test to correctly say you have it?
  • Specificity (True Negative Rate): If you do not have the disease, how likely is the test to correctly say you don't?

Imagine a rare disease that affects 1% of the population.

You go to the doctor and take a test for this disease. The test is excellent: it has 99% Sensitivity (it catches 99% of sick people) and 99% Specificity (it correctly clears 99% of healthy people).

Your test result comes back positive. What is the actual probability that you have the disease?

How does this work?

This is Bayes' Theorem in action, demonstrating the Base Rate Fallacy. Even with a highly accurate test, when a disease is rare (the base rate), most positive results are actually false alarms.

Imagine 1,000 people. If 1% have the disease (10 people), a 99% sensitive test correctly identifies ~10 of them (True Positives).

But there are 990 healthy people. A 99% specific test means it will be wrong 1% of the time for healthy people. 1% of 990 is ~10 people (False Positives).

So out of ~20 total positive results, only ~10 actually have the disease. That means your chance of being sick if you test positive is only 50%!

Now drop prevalence to 0.1% using the sliders. Only 1 person is sick. The test still produces ~10 false positives among the 999 healthy people. So P(Disease|Positive) drops to about 9%. This is why mass screening for extremely rare conditions often causes more unnecessary stress than benefit.
