Probability Paradoxes

Interactive simulations that reveal how our intuitions about probability go spectacularly wrong. Play each game, then watch the math prove you wrong.

🚪 Monty Hall Problem You're on a game show. You pick a door. The host opens a different door to reveal a goat. Now you can stay or switch. Does it matter which you choose? Game 🎂 Birthday Paradox How many people need to be in a room before there's a better-than-even chance that two of them share the same birthday? Your gut says a lot. Reality disagrees sharply. Simulation 🧪 False Positive Paradox A test for a rare disease is 99% accurate. You test positive. How confident should you be that you actually have the disease? The answer defies the obvious. Visualization 📊 Simpson's Paradox Can Treatment A outperform Treatment B in every individual trial, yet still lose when the results are combined? Most people say that's mathematically impossible. Data Explorer 😴 Sleeping Beauty Problem A fair coin is flipped while you sleep. Depending on the result, you may be woken once or twice with your memory erased in between. When you wake up, what should you believe about the coin? Debate 📦 Bertrand's Box Paradox Three boxes contain two gold coins, two silver coins, or one of each. You draw a gold coin from a random box. What are the odds the other coin in that box is also gold? Game 🪙 St. Petersburg Paradox A casino offers a game: flip a coin until tails, and your payout doubles with each heads. The entry fee is up to you. How much would a rational person pay to play? Game 🔢 Benford's Law In large real-world datasets — populations, prices, election results — the leading digits are not evenly distributed. This quirk is so reliable it's used to detect fraud. Explorer 💼 Secretary Problem You interview candidates one by one and must hire or reject each on the spot — no going back. What's the mathematically optimal strategy to find the best person? Strategy 🎰 Penney's Game Both players choose a 3-flip coin sequence. You flip until one appears. Since a fair coin is used, it should be 50/50 — right? Pick your sequence first and find out. Game 🔒 100 Prisoners Riddle 100 prisoners. 100 boxes. Each must find their own number by opening only 50 boxes. If anyone fails, they all die. Can a pre-agreed strategy give them a realistic chance?
