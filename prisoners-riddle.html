100 Prisoners Riddle

100 prisoners. 100 boxes. Each must find their own number by opening only 50 boxes. Can a pre-agreed strategy give them a real fighting chance?

Before you simulate — what do you think?

100 prisoners are each numbered 1–100. Their numbers are written on slips of paper, placed randomly into 100 numbered boxes in a locked room. Each prisoner enters alone, opens at most 50 boxes, and must find their own number. They cannot communicate once the process starts, but may agree on a strategy beforehand. If even one prisoner fails, they all die. What is the best possible collective survival probability?

How does this work?

The rules are brutal: 100 prisoners, 100 boxes containing slips of paper numbered 1-100 randomly. Each prisoner can open 50 boxes to find their own number. They cannot communicate after starting. If everyone finds their number, they live. If one fails, they all die.

With Random Guessing, each prisoner has a 1/2 chance. The chance of ALL 100 succeeding is (1/2)100, essentially zero.

The Pointer Strategy: Prisoner X opens box X. If it contains number Y, they next open box Y. They follow this chain until they find their number X. This leverages mathematical permutations.

Why does it work? Random shuffling creates permutation cycles. If the longest cycle is 50 or less, EVERY prisoner will find their number! The math shows there's a ~31.1% chance that a random permutation contains no cycle longer than 50. Thus, their survival rate jumps from zero to ~31%!
