Simpson's Paradox

Which treatment is better?

A hospital is testing two different kidney stone treatments. They classify patients into two groups: those with Small Stones (mild) and Large Stones (severe). Here are the results:

Small Stones

  • Treatment A: 93% success rate (81 / 87 patients)
  • Treatment B: 87% success rate (234 / 270 patients)

Large Stones

  • Treatment A: 73% success rate (192 / 263 patients)
  • Treatment B: 69% success rate (55 / 80 patients)

Treatment A is better for Small Stones. It is ALSO better for Large Stones.

If we combine all the patients together into one overall group, which treatment do you think has the higher overall success rate?

How does this work?

Simpson's Paradox occurs when a trend appearing in separate groups reverses when the groups are combined. This happens because of a lurking variable — the group sizes are severely unbalanced between treatments.

In this classic kidney stone data, Treatment A is used much more often on large stones (the harder cases with low success rates). Treatment B is used mostly on small stones (easy cases with high success rates).

Treatment A is objectively the better treatment for both types of stones. But because Treatment A takes on almost all the difficult cases, its overall success rate gets dragged down. Treatment B gets to coast on lots of easy cases, artificially inflating its overall score.

The paradox warns us that aggregated data can be highly misleading. Always consider whether a confounding variable might be hiding the true relationship.
