How does this work?

In the St. Petersburg game, you flip a fair coin repeatedly until it lands tails. If the first tails appears on flip n, you win $2n.

The expected value of this game is infinite: each flip contributes $1 to the expected payout (probability 1/2n times payout 2n = $1), and there are infinitely many possible flips.

Yet most rounds pay very little — you get $2 half the time, $4 a quarter of the time, and so on. The paradox is that despite the infinite expected value, no rational person would pay a huge amount to play, because most of the expected value comes from astronomically rare events.

Watch the running average as you play: it trends upward over time, never settling on a fixed number. Occasionally a big win spikes the average, illustrating how rare outliers dominate the expectation.
