Why 1/3, not 1/2?

When two children are born, there are four equally likely outcomes: Boy+Boy, Boy+Girl, Girl+Boy, Girl+Girl (each with 25% probability).

The clue "at least one is a boy" eliminates only one outcome — Girl+Girl. That leaves three equally likely cases: BB, BG, GB. Only one of those three is Boy+Boy, so the probability is 1/3.

The trap: many people reason "one child is already a boy, so the other has a 50% chance." But the clue doesn't tell you which child is the boy — it tells you something about the family, which restricts the whole sample space.

Variant: If instead you're told "the older child is a boy," that pins down one specific child. Now the sample space is just Boy+Boy and Boy+Girl — two cases — making the probability 1/2. The wording genuinely changes the math.
